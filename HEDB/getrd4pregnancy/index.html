Gametogensis

Gametogensis has 4 phases:

  • The germ cells which is extraembronic origined migrate into the gonads. This process is same for both male and female.
  • Mitosis of the germ cells, making an increase in the number.
  • Meiosis of the germ cells, making a reduction in chomosomal number.
  • Structural and functional maturation of the eggs and spermatozoa.

Phase 1 Migration

  1. Precursors of gametes arise outside the gonads;
  2. These cells can be recongnizable at 24d after fertilization;
  3. These precursors are located in the endodermal layer of the yolk sac
  4. They have characteristics as below:
  • Large size,
  • High content of glycogen,
  • Hign content of alkaline phosphatase.

The process of Migration:

  • Germ cells exit from the yolk sac
  • Get into the hindgut epithelium
  • Migrate through the dorsal mesentery
  • Reach the primordia of the gonads

If primordial germ cells lodge in extragonadal sites and survived, they become teratomas.

Phase 2 Mitosis

Oogonia: mitotically active germ cells in the female.

  1. Intense mitotic activity: a few hundreds to 7 Mil.
  2. Atresia: natural degeneration until menopause.

Spermatogonia: mitotically active germ cells in the male.

  1. Mitosis begins in the embryotic testes
  2. Mitosis ceases in the second trimester
  3. Mitosis resumes at puberty
  4. Mitosis continues throughout life

Phase 3 Meiosis

Biological significance:

  • Reduction of the number of chromosomes. (from diloid to haploid)
  • Independent reassortment of maternal and paternal chromosomes.
  • Further redistribution of genetic material through the process of cross-over.

Meiosis in females

Primary oocyte: the oogonia enter the first meiotic division

Primary oocyte arrests in the prophase of the first meiotic division

Primary oocyte accumulates mRNA, rRNA, and cortical granules

Primary oocytes (10-30) complete the first meiotic division with each menstrual cycle after puberty

After completion, secondary oocyte and polar body appear.

The secondary oocytes begin the second meiotic division, and are arrested at metaphase

The secondary oocytes complete the second meiotic division when it is fertilized by a spermatozoon

A fertilized ovum and second polar body are produced

Meiosis in males

Meiosis begins at puberty

Small numbers of spermatogonia enter the meiotic cycle as primary spermatocytes

The first meiotic division produces two secondary spermatocytes

The second meiotic division produces four spermatids

The total length of human spermatogenesis is approximately 74 days

Phase 4 Maturation (Structural and Functional)

Oogenesis

Follicle: the combination of oocyte and follicular cells

Three important elements in oocyte growth and maturation:

  • Cytoplasm preparation—-organelles, gene products
  • Asymmetric distribution of cytoplasmic components (especially cytoskeleton)
  • Extensive cross-talk between the oocyte and follicular cells

Primordial follicles: primary oocytes with few cells

Primary follicle: primary oocytes with complete layer of follicular cells (granulosa cells)

Secondary follicle: primary oocytes with more than one layer of surrounding granulosa cells

Antral follicle: contains an antrum filled with fluid

Tertiary (graafian) follicle: enlarged follicle presses again the surface of the ovary

Growth and maturation of a follicle along with major endocrine interactions in the theca cells and granulosa cells

Zona pellucida: prominent, translucent, noncellular membrane (blue color)

Secondary follicle has two layers:

  • Membrana granulosa: form a barrier (purple)
  • Epithelial granulosa cells

Theca folliculi: derived from the ovarian connective tissue

  • Theca interna : highly vascularized and glandular cell
  • Theca externa: connective tissue-like capsule

After antrum formation:

Cumulus cells: immediately surrounding the oocyte

Mural granulosa cells: between the antrum and the membrana granulosa

E, Estrogen; FSH, follicle-stimulating hormone; LH, luteinizing hormone; R, receptor; T, testosterone.

The principal hormonal stimulus for ovulation is LH.

As puberty approaches,

  1. Granulosa cell develops FSH receptors
  2. Pituitary FSH acts on granulosa cells
  3. Granulosa cells produce small amounts of estrogens
  4. Antrum forms
  5. Theca interna possess LH receptors
  6. Theca interna cells produce androgens
  7. Androgens pass through the membrana granulosa to granulosacells
  8. FSH induces the granulosa cells to synthesize the enzyme (aromatase)
  9. Aromatase converts androgens into estrogens
  10. Estrogens stimulates the formation of LH receptors on the granulosa cells

Major steps leading to meiotic arrest in the embryonic oocyte

Cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP), contributed by both the oocyte and follicular cells, inactivates maturation promoting factor (MPF)

Meiotic arrest

Cyclic guanosine monophosphate (cGMP) from the follicular cells inactivates phosphodiesterase 3A (PDE3A),

The Inhibition of PDE3A prevents from breaking down cAMP molecules, and allowing a high concentration of cAMP in the oocyte

Meiotic arrest

The resumption of meiosis in response to the LH surge

  1. Under the influence of luteinizing hormone (LH), gap junctions of the cumulus cells close down
  2. It reduces the amount of both cAMP and cGMP that is transferred from the cumulus cells to the oocyte
  3. The reduction in cGMP activates PDE3A
  4. PDE3A breaks down cAMP within the oocyte
  5. The lowered concentration of cAMP within the oocyte activates MPF
  6. MPF stimulates resumption of meiosis
Apoptosis: the mechanism for the loss of germ cells and oocytes during embryogenesis

Germ cells are particularly susceptible to apoptosis during three periods of oogenesis:

  1. During the shift from mitosis to meiosis
  2. During the progression through prophase I—due to defective DNA crossover repair
  3. During the formation of primary follicles—due to the degeneration or abnormal function of follicular cells

Spermatogenesis

Spermatogonia
Type A spermatogonia: stem cell, mantain the ability to mitosis.
Type B spermatogonia: enter meiosis.

Sertoli cell: supporting cell
Blood-testis barrier: The Sertoli cell processes are joined and form an immunological barrier

The type B spermatogonia enter the meiotic cycle as primary spermatocytes.
The first meiotic division produces two secondary spermatocytes. .
The second meiotic division produces four spermatids (Haploid gametes)(They are connected. After spermiogenesis, they will form spermatozoa).

Cellular preparation:

  1. Production of variety of RNA and storage
  2. Production of protamines:
    • arginine-rich, cysteine-rich proteins
    • displace the lysine-rich histones
    • allow highly degree of compaction of chromatin
    • its mRNA is translated at spermatid stage
  3. Production of small RNAs
    • stabilizing other RNAs
    • epigenetic regulation of early development
  4. After the compaction of the chromatin, both transcription of RNA and translational activity ceases
Coordination between release of mature spermatids and the dissolution and reconstruction of the blood-testis barrier.
  1. With degradation of the surface adhesion complex, mature spermatids are released into the lumen of the seminiferous tubule.
  2. Active laminin fragments join with cytokines and proteinases to begin to degrade the junctional proteins at the blood-testis barrier located apically to the late type B spermatogonium.
  3. The old blood-testis barrier breaks down.
  4. Under the influence of testosterone, a new blood-testis barrier forms basal to what is now a preleptotene primary spermatocyte.

 

BTB, Blood-testis barrier; EI°S, early primary spermatocyte; ESt, early spermatid; LI°S, late primary spermatocyte; LSt, late spermatid; S-A, type A spermatogonium; S-B, type B spermatogonium; St, spermatid.

Summary of the major stages in spermiogenesis

Spermiogenesis: the process of transformation from spermatids to spermatozoa

  • Progressive reduction in the size of the nucleus
  • Tremendous condensation of the chromosomal material
  • Reorganization of the cytoplasm
  • Condensation of the Golgi apparatus gives rise to the acrosome
  • Flagellum grows out the centriolar region
  • Mitochondria are arranged in a spiral around the proximal part of the flagellum
  • The residual bodies are phagocytized by Sertoli cells
The structure of a mature spermatozoon.

Head: the nucleus and acrosome
Midpiece:

  • the centrioles
  • the proximal part of the flagellum
  • the mitochondrial helix
    The tail: highly specialized flagellum

Preperation for Pregnancy

Female

Uterine Tubes

  1. Fimbriae and Infundibulum
    • Structure: finger-like projections and their opening
    • Function: directing the ovulated egg into the tube
  2. Ampulla
    • Structure: high density of prominent longitudinal folds
    • Function: assisting in gamete transport and supporting the early development of the embryo
  3. Isthmus
    • Structure: less dense longitudinal folds and thicker smooth muscles
    • Function: serving as a site of temporary sperm storage and participating in the final stage of functional maturation of sperm cells
  4. Intramural
    • Structure: thin lumen containing mucus
    • Function: regulating the passage of spermatozoa into the uterine tube and restricting the entry of bacteria into the tube

Uterus

  1. Myometrium
    • Structure: smooth muscles
    • Function: expel the fetus
  2. Endometrium
    • Structure: the mucosal lining, changes daily throughou the menstrual cycle
      • Function layer: shed with each menstrual period
      • Basal layer: remains intact
    • Function: receive and maintain the embryo during pregnancy
  3. Microscopic structure of endometrium
    • Columnar surface epithelium
    • Uterine glands
    • Specialized connective tissue stroma
    • Spiral arteries
  4. Cervix
    • Structure: not typical uterine endometrium, but is studded with irregular crypts
    • Function: produces glycoprotein-rich cervical mucus to regulate the spermatozoa to penetrate the cervix

Hormonal Control of the Female Reproductive Cycle

Hypothalamic Control: first level

  • Produce gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH)
  • Act on the anterior lobe of the pituitary gland to stimulate the secretion of LH and FSH

Pituitary Gland Control: second level

  • Anterior pituitary: FSH, LH, and prolactin
  • Posterior pituitary: oxytocin

Ovaries and Placenta Control: third level

  • Ovaries’ granulosa cells convert androgens into estrogens
  • After ovulation, progesterone is the principal product
  • During later pregnancy, placenta synthesize estrogens, progesterone, human chorionic gonadotropin (HCG), and Human placental lactogen

Reproductive Target Tissues: fourth level

  • Ciliated cells, smooth muscle, endometrial lining of the uterus

Proliferative phase (day 5-14)
Under the influence of estrogens

  1. The uterine lining is re-epithelialized
  2. The endometrial stroma progressively thickens
  3. The uterine glands elongate
  4. The spial arteries begin to grow toward the surface
  5. The mucous glands of the cervix secrete glycoprotein-rich but watery mucus

At the end of proliferative phase (day 12-13)
Under the influence of estrogens

  1. A surge in the hypothalamic secretion of GnRH
  2. A surge of LH and FSH by the pituitary gland

Secretory phase (day 15-28)
Under the influence of LH

  1. Ovulation
  2. The graafian follicle becomes corpus luteum
  3. The granulosa cells produce progesterone

Under the influence of progesterone

  1. The uterine tube epithelium secrets nutrition for the embryo
  2. Induce regression of some of the ciliated cells in the tubal epithelium

If pregnancy occurs
Estrogen

  1. The endometrium has thickened
  2. The straight uterine glands begin to coil
  3. The spiral arteries grow farther toward the endometrial surface

Progesterone

  1. The cervical mucus becomes highly viscous and acts as a protective block
  2. The vaginal epithelium becomes thinner
  3. Development of the secretory components of the mammary glands

If pregnancy does not occur

  1. The granulosa cells produce protein inhibin
  2. Inhibin inhibits FSH secretion and reduces LH secretion
  3. Regression of the corpus luteum
  4. Reduction in the secretion of progesterone
  5. The constriction and breakdown of the spiral arteries that cause local ischemia
  6. The ischemia results in local hemorrhage and loss of integrity of areas of the endometrium

Quiz

  1. When does meiosis begin in the female and in the male?

In the female, meiosis begins before or at birth. In the male, meiosis begin after puberty.

  1. What is the difference between spermatogenesis and spermiogenesis?

Spermatogenesis means the whole process of the meiosis and the cellular preparation.

Spermiogenesis means a part of spermatogenesis which is the process of transformation from spermatids to spermatozoa.

  1. At what two stages of oogenesis in meiosis arrested in the female?

The first stage is from the birth to the puberty. The meiotic division arrests in diplotene state.

The second stage is after ovulation and before the fertilization. The meiosis arrest at metaphase II.
