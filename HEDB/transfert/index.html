HEDB | 2 Transport of Gametes and Fertilization

Ovulation; Egg and Sperm Transport; Fertilization

Transport

Ovulation

  • The ovum
  • The zona pellucida
  • The two- to three-cell thick corona radiata
  • A sticky matrix containing surrounding cells of the cumulus oophorus

Egg Transport

Capture:

  1. The fimbriae of the uterine tube move closer to the ovary and sweep over
  2. The cilia of the epithelial cells of the uterine tube create current
  3. Adhesive interaction between the egg complex and the ciliary surface of the tube
  4. Transport in two phases: by smooth muscle contraction
  5. Slow transport in the ampulla: 72 hours
  6. Rapid transport in isthmus to uterus: 8 hours

Sperm Transport

Step 1: the spermatozoa are morphologically mature but nonmotile and incapable of fertilizing an egg
Step 2: the spermatozoa are passively moved to epididymis for maturation. After two weeks, they are capable of fertilizing an egg
Step 3-5: on ejaculation, the spermatozoa are mixed with fluid secretions from the seminal vesicles and prostate gland
The seminal vesicle fluid is rich in fructose and prostaglandins
Prostatic fluid is rich in citric acid and phosphatase, zinc and magnesium ions

Semen: 2-6 ml, pH 7.2-7.8, > 100 million sperms

Sperm transport in the female reproductive tract

Step 1:

  • pH adjustment (from 4.3 to 7.2)
  • Semen coagulates into a gel with semenogelin

Step 2:

Cervical canal and the cervical mucus (cervical mucin) barriers

  • E mucus: watery mucus facilitates the passage of sperm (day 9-16)
  • G mucus: sticky mucus resists to sperm penetration (after ovulation)

Immunological barriers

  • Cells: neutrophils and macrophages
    Molecules: IgA, IgG, complement
    Two modes of sperm transport through the cervix
  • Fast phase: 5-20 mins, due to muscular movements, cannot fertilizing an egg
  • Slow phase: 2-4 days, due to the motility of the spermatozoa themselves

Step 3:

  • Swimming movements of sperm
  • Uterine musculature contraction

Step 4: How to choose the uterine tubes with the ovulated egg

  • The ovulated egg side of tube carries hormones
  • The temperature of the ovulated egg side of tube is 1.6°C higher
  • Spermatozoa have odorant receptor: attractants

Step 5: Functional maturation: the spermatozoa bind to the epithelium for 24 hours in isthmus

  • The spermatozoa undergo the capacitation reaction
    • Removal of cholesterol from the surface of the sperm
    • Removal of glycoproteins on the surface of the spermatozoa
  • The spermatozoa undergo a period of hyperactivity
    • Break free from the epithelium
    • Assist the sperm in penetrating the corona radiata and the zona pellucida

Step 6:

  • Fertilization of the egg occurs in the ampullary portion

Overall guidance to the egg:

  • The spermatozoa possess odorant receptors
  • The spermatozoa respond to cumulus-derived progesterone
  • The spermatozoa respond to a temperature gradient

Formation and Function of the Corpus Luteum (CL) of Ovulation and Pregnancy

If fertilization does not occur

Luteolysis: CL deteriorates

  • Apoptosis
  • Prostaglandin F2

Reduction in progesterone

Corpus albicans: collagenous scar tissue

If fertilization occurs

Chorionic gonadotropin:

  • Maintain its functional condition
  • Increase in size
  • Increase hormone production

Mainly theca lutein cells

Placenta hormones replace it later

After ovulation:

  • The basement membrane breaks down
  • Thecal blood vessels to grow into the cavity of - the raptured follicle
  • The granulosa cells become granulosa lutein cells
  • They secret progesterone and estrogen for 10 days
  • Corpus luteum forms

Fertilization

Penetration of the Corona Radiata: (A) and (B)

Structure of the outer layer of egg complex:

  • The corona radiata
  • Intercellular matrix
  • High concentration of carbohydrates—hyaluronic acid

Penetration of this layer:

  • Hyaluronidase from the sperm head
  • Active swimming of the sperm

The zona pellucida consists of four glycoproteins – ZP1, ZP2, ZP3 and ZP4.

Attachment to the zona pellucida: (C)

Attachment:

Spermatozoa bind to a sialic acid molecule of the O-linked oligosaccharides that are attached to the ZP3

Spermatozoa have specific ZP3 sperm receptor

Sperm binding regions of the ZP3 molecule varies among species

Penetration of the zona pellucida: (D)

Penetration:

Spermatozoa undergo the acrosomal reaction

  1. A large segment of the polypeptide chain of the ZP3 acts through G proteins in the plasma membrane on the sperm head
  2. A massive influx of Ca2+ with an influx of Na+ and an efflux H+,
  3. Intracellular pH increases
  4. The fusion of parts of the outer acrosomal membrane with the overlying plasma membrane
  5. The pinching off of fused parts as small vesicles
  6. The liberation of the multitude of lytic enzymes
  7. The inner acrosomal membrane fuses with the remaining postacrosomal plasma membrane
  8. The sperm is in perivitelline space to directly contact with egg membrane

Binding and Fusion of Spermatozoon and Egg: (E) and (F)

Binding:

  • Spermatozoa attach to region of the egg’s surface containing microvilli
  • The region of the first polar body and the metaphase spindle does not have microvilli
  • Izumo (the sperm protein) and CD9 (the egg membrane protein) are involved in binding

Fusion:

  • The sperm’s plasma membrane incorporates into egg’s plasma membrane
  • The sperm head, the midpiece, and the tail sink into egg.
  • The paternal mitochondria in the fertilized egg are eliminated

Prevention of Polyspermy

Only one sperm is allowed to penetrate the oocyte

Two mechanisms ensure monospermy:

Fast block to polyspermy (in sea urchins)

  • On contact of 1st sperm, Na+ diffuses into the oocyte from extracellular space
  • Membrane depolarization (from -70 to +10 mV): prevents addition sperm from fusing with the oocyte membrane

Slow block to polyspermy

  • On sperm entry, Ca2+ released by oocyte endoplasmic reticulum as part of preparation for cell division
  • Zona reaction: cortical granules beneath the plasma membrane rupture contents (polysaccharides and zonal inhibiting proteins)into perivitelline space
  • Polysaccharides become hydrated and swell, thus causing the zona pellucida to rise
  • ZIP hydrolyze and destroy the sperm receptor molecules (ZP3)

Completion of Meiosis and Development of Pronuclei in the Egg

Before fertilization, the nucleus of the egg arrested in metaphase of the second meiotic division

After penetration by the sperm, the nucleus of the egg completes the second division and releases a second polar body

The mitotic spindle contractions of the actin–myosin complex pull on either end of the mitotic spindle (red arrows). On the end of the spindle closest to the cell surface, the intensity of the pull is greater (thick red arrow), and the entire spindle apparatus moves toward that surface

As the mitotic process nears completion, one daughter nucleus buds off from the oocyte as a polar body

Summary of the main events involved in fertilization

Zygote formation

After both sperm and egg pronuclei formed, DNA replication occurs and each chromosome forms two chromatids

When the sperm and egg pronuclei join, their chromosomes become organized for a mitotic division.

Fertilization is complete and the fertilized egg is called a zygote

Quiz

  1. What is the principal hormonal stimulus for ovulation?

Estrogen(E) which comes from testosterone(T), follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH), luteinizing hormone (LH).

  1. What is capacitation?

In isthmus, sperms stay for about 24h for the final stage of functional maturation. Before the capacitation, sperm can’t participate in fertilization.

The cholesterol and glycoproteins on the surface of the sperm are removed.

  1. What is polyspermy, and how is it prevented after a spermatozoon enters the egg?

Polyspermy means more than one sperm fertilize the egg, which causes the number of chromosomes goes wrong. There are two mechanisms to prevent polyspermy.

In the fast block, once the first sperm contact with the egg, Na+ diffuses into the egg, making membrane depolarization. That prevents any more sperms fuse with the egg.

In the slow block, once the sperm entry, Ca2+ released by oocyte endoplasmic reticulum. Also cortical granules beneath the plasma membrane diffuse into perivitelline space, which has two effect. One is Polysaccharides become hydrated and swell, causing the zona pellucida to rise. The other is ZIP hydrolyze, destroying ZP3.
